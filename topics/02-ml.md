# ML Basics

Just enough context for everything that follows. This chapter is not a crash course in machine learning — it is the shortest path to understanding the four concepts the rest of the talk leans on: tokens, the context window, the Transformer, and how a model like Claude actually gets built.

## The evolution of AI

AI did not arrive fully formed in 2022. Three eras got us here.

**The symbolic era (1950s–1980s).** Rule-based AI. `if-then` statements, all the way down. The first chess engines lived here. The program could not learn — it could only follow a script humans had written for it.

**The machine-learning shift (1990s–2010s).** The industry stopped trying to give computers rules and started giving them data. Neural networks — loose mathematical analogues of the human brain — learned to recognize patterns from examples rather than being programmed with them directly.

**The Wall (RNNs and LSTMs).** Before 2017, AI read text the way a human does: word by word. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their longer-memory cousins, LSTMs, passed information forward through the sequence one step at a time. It worked, but it was slow, and the model tended to forget the beginning of a long sentence by the time it reached the end.

**The 2017 breakthrough.** The Google paper *Attention Is All You Need* introduced the Transformer, and everything changed.

## The Wall, in more detail

It is worth spending another minute on why the RNN era felt like hitting a literal ceiling. Three failures kept stacking on top of each other.

**1. The vanishing gradient (the fading memory).** RNNs process data in a chain. To understand word #20, the model has to pass information all the way from word #1 through words #2, #3, and so on. By the time the signal reached the end of a long paragraph, the information from the beginning had *vanished* — mathematically, the numbers became so small they rounded to zero. The AI would forget the subject of a sentence by the time it reached the verb.

**2. The sequential bottleneck (the speed wall).** Because RNNs read word by word, you *cannot* parallelize the work. Imagine you have 1,000 powerful GPUs — with an RNN, you cannot use all of them at once to read a book because the model must finish word #1 before it can even look at word #2. This made training on the scale of the internet basically impossible. Too slow, too expensive.

**3. The lack of global context.** Old AI was like a person reading a book through a tiny keyhole. It could see what was right in front of it, but it could not look back at a previous chapter to understand a metaphor. No global perspective.

The Transformer threw away the chain entirely. It looks at every word in a 10,000-word document at the same time. Massive GPU fleets could finally be used in parallel, and distance no longer mattered — word #1 and word #10,000 were mathematically "next" to each other through the **attention** mechanism.

## The Transformer

> **Spotlight analogy.** Old AI was a flashlight moving across a page. The Transformer is a floodlight that illuminates the whole room at once.

Instead of reading linearly, a Transformer processes the entire sequence at once. And the way it understands *what each word means in context* is a mechanism called **self-attention**.

### Self-attention

Self-attention is the brain of the LLM. It lets the model decide, for each word, how much every other word should influence its meaning.

Example: *"The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired."* Attention is what lets the model know "it" refers to the animal, not the street.

Multiply that decision across every word in a sentence, across every sentence in a paragraph, across every layer of the network, and you have something that can build up a genuine contextual understanding of a full document.

### Masked self-attention

LLMs generate text one token at a time, left to right. They are *autoregressive*. When the decoder is predicting the next token, it must only attend to tokens that have already been generated — never to future ones.

This is enforced with a **mask**: a filter applied during the attention calculation that hides every future position from the model's view.

The mask exists to prevent the model from cheating during training. The model is fed a full known sentence — "The cat sat on the mat" — and asked to predict each next word. Without the mask, while training to predict "sat" it would already see "on the mat" and just learn to copy whatever came next. The mask forces it to make predictions based only on the prefix it has seen, which is exactly how it operates when generating new text for real.

You cannot remove the mask from a standard autoregressive decoder without collapsing the training process. It is not an optional component — it is what turns a sequence *processor* into a sequence *generator*.

## Tokens: the currency of AI

Think of tokens as the Lego blocks of language. LLMs do not read letters or words — they read chunks of characters.

A token can be a whole word (`apple`), part of a word (`ing`), or even a single punctuation mark. Rough conversion: **1,000 tokens ≈ 750 words**.

Tokens matter because processing text costs money and compute. If a model has a vocabulary of 50,000 tokens, it has a lookup table where every token is assigned a unique number. High-quality tokenizers (like the one Claude uses) are more efficient at packing information. A single good token might represent a complex medical term; a bad tokenizer would break that same term into ten pieces.

## The context window: working memory

The context window is the maximum amount of information the model can hold in its head at any given time.

Imagine the AI has a desk. The context window is the size of that desk. Once the desk is full of papers (tokens), adding a new one pushes the oldest one off the edge, and it is forgotten.

Claude is famous for a massive context window — up to 200,000 tokens, roughly the length of a 500-page book.

Why not make it infinite? **Quadratic complexity.** In a standard Transformer, if you double the context window, the compute cost increases *four times* — O(n²). Managing a 200k-token window requires incredibly optimized hardware and software, and the cost curve climbs fast. Even when you have the room, you pay for every token on every turn.

## Anatomy of an LLM

An LLM is not just a Transformer. It is a stack of systems, with the Transformer at its core.

> **The building analogy.** The embedding layer is the lobby where you check in. The Transformer layers are the floors where the work gets done. The softmax is the exit where the answer comes out. The more floors (layers), the more complex the "thinking."

### Embedding layer
The entry point. It turns your text into a list of numbers (vectors) in a high-dimensional space where related meanings sit close to each other.

### Positional encodings
Transformers process all words at once, so they do not naturally know word order. This layer adds time-stamps to each token so the model knows "The dog bit the man" is different from "The man bit the dog."

### Transformer layers (the brain)
Most modern LLMs are **decoder-only** Transformers — tall stacks of identical layers, each containing:

- **Multi-head attention.** Multiple attention heads look at different parts of the sequence simultaneously. One head might focus on grammar while another tracks the relationship between names and dates.
- **Feed-forward network (FFN).** After attention, this layer processes the weighted information and extracts higher-level meaning.

### Layer normalization
Acts like a thermostat, keeping the mathematical signals from getting too large or too small as they travel through 50+ layers.

### Softmax output
The final step that converts the model's internal math back into a probability distribution — predicting which token is most likely to come next.

## Training

The architecture above is just wiring. The *knowledge* inside a model comes from training.

**Pre-training.** The model reads the entire internet — books, code, articles, forums — to learn grammar, facts, and reasoning from statistical patterns across billions of documents.

**RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback).** Humans rank model answers, and that signal is used to teach the model to be helpful and safe. This is the step that turns a raw language predictor into something you would actually want to talk to.

## Does Claude have a unique architecture?

Anthropic is famously secretive about its exact wiring, but a few things are known publicly.

**Constitutional AI (CAI).** Not a physical layer, but a training architecture. Instead of learning only from humans, Claude is trained against a *Constitution* — a list of principles. It critiques its own drafts during training to ensure they are safe and helpful.

**MoE (Mixture of Experts) — rumored.** It is widely suspected that larger Claude models use a Mixture-of-Experts design. Instead of one giant brain processing every request, the model is split into 16 or 32 "experts." For a math question, only the math-expert layers activate. This makes the model faster and smarter without running the whole thing every time.

**Multimodal from the ground up.** Claude 3 was built as a multimodal model, not retrofitted. Its architecture treats image pixels almost like text tokens, letting it see and read at the same time.
